## The Shift: From "Can I Build It?" to "Should I Build It?"

The fundamental question facing indie builders has completely changed.

**The Old Bottleneck (2015-2022):**
- "Can I build this?"
- "Do I have the technical skills?"
- "Can I afford to hire developers?"
- "How long will it take to ship?"

**The New Bottleneck (2023-2025):**

**Building is no longer the constraint. Understanding problems is.**

If anything, it made it harder. Because now you can build so fast that the temptation to skip validation is overwhelming.

## What Actually Cuts Through the Noise

Here's what I've learned after those three failures:

**Users don't need more products. They need solutions to genuine problems.**

And "genuine problems" have specific characteristics:

### 1. They're Painful Enough to Motivate Change

**Not genuine:**
- "It's kind of annoying to manually post to Twitter"
- "I wish my notes were slightly better organized"
- "It would be nice if this was 10% faster"

**Genuine:**
- "I'm spending $5,000/month on contractors doing manual work"
- "We missed three client deadlines because of coordination failures"  
- "I wasted 6 months building products nobody used"

**The difference:** Severity. Real problems cost real money, time, or dignity.

### 2. They're Frequent Enough to Matter

**Not genuine:**
- "Once last year I had trouble with..."
- "Occasionally I run into..."
- "Sometimes it's annoying when..."

**Genuine:**
- "Every single day I waste 2 hours on..."
- "Three times this week we've had to..."
- "This has cost me the last four projects..."

**The difference:** Frequency. Real problems happen often enough to demand attention.

### 3. Current Solutions Are Clearly Insufficient

**Not genuine:**
- "Existing tools work fine, but yours has a nicer UI"
- "I could do this manually, but automation would be cool"
- "I use [competitor], it's okay I guess"

**Genuine:**
- "I've tried five different tools, none of them work"
- "I'm paying $500/month for a solution I hate"
- "We built an internal tool because nothing exists"

**The difference:** Desperation. Real problems drive people to extreme measures.

## The Framework: Jobs to Be Done

The methodology that changed everything for me is called **Jobs to Be Done** (JTBD).

It's not new. Bob Moesta developed it decades ago. But it's more relevant now than ever.

The core insight: **People don't buy products. They hire products to make progress.**

When someone "hires" your product, they're trying to move from a current state (struggling) to a desired state (better).

**Four forces determine if they'll hire you:**

### PUSH (Pain of Current State)
How much does their current situation suck?
- Weak: "It's fine, a bit annoying"
- Strong: "This is costing me $10K/month and causing stress"

### PULL (Attraction to New Solution)  
How appealing is your solution?
- Weak: "That sounds interesting I guess"
- Strong: "This is exactly what I need, I'd pay $100/month"

### ANXIETY (Fear of New Solution)
What makes them hesitate?
- Low: "Just need to see it working"
- High: "Last three tools failed, data was lost, can't risk it"

### HABIT (Inertia of Current Solution)
How hard is it to switch?
- Low: "Currently do it manually, would love to stop"
- High: "Team trained on current tool, 2 years of data, integrated everywhere"

**The magic:** You can measure these forces. And they predict success.

**The builders who get this will win.** Not because they build faster. Because they build the right things.

## What This Means for You

If you're building something right now, ask yourself:

**Can you describe the problem your product solves in a way that makes someone go "YES, that's exactly my struggle"?**

If not, you haven't understood the problem well enough yet.

**Can you quantify how painful the problem is? How often it happens? What it costs?**

If not, you don't know if the problem is severe enough to motivate switching.

**Can you explain why existing solutions fail? Why someone would leave a working tool for yours?**

If not, you haven't found a strong enough pull or weak enough habit force.

**Can you list the specific anxieties preventing someone from trying your solution?**

If not, you'll wonder why people say they're interested but never sign up.

## A Challenge

Before you build your next product, try this:

**Step 1:** Interview 10 people who have the problem you're solving
- Not friends. Not family. People who actually have the problem.
- Ask about their struggles, not your solution
- Measure the four forces (PUSH, PULL, ANXIETY, HABIT)

**Step 2:** Calculate your build signal
- Add PUSH + PULL (must be 140+ for strong signal)
- Subtract ANXIETY + HABIT (lower is better)
- Only build if the combined signal is strong

**Step 3:** If signal is strong, build with validated insights
- Use customer language in your copy
- Address anxieties explicitly
- Emphasize outcomes they mentioned
- Design for the job they're trying to do

**Step 4:** If signal is weak, try a different idea
- You just saved 2 months
- Validate another idea in 3 days
- Repeat until you find strong signal
